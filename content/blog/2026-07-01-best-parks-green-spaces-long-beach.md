Long Beach runs one of the largest municipal park systems in Southern California — more than 170 parks spread across roughly 3,200 acres, from an 800-acre regional wilderness on the east side to a mile-long bluff walk above the Pacific. For anyone trying to live a healthier, more active life here, that green infrastructure is the single best free resource in the city. You don't need a gym membership when you have a nature-center loop, a running track, a fitness course, and a bluff-top sunset walk all within a few miles of home.

Here's our guide to the best parks and green spaces in Long Beach, organized so you can match the right park to the kind of movement you're after — quiet trail time, a hard workout, or a walk with a view.

## El Dorado in East Long Beach: The Regional Anchor

If you only visit one park, make it **El Dorado East Regional Park**. This 800-plus-acre complex on the east side of Long Beach, split by the San Gabriel River, is the closest thing the city has to a wilderness escape without leaving the 90815. Inside it you'll find fishing lakes, miles of bike trails, an archery range that offers free lessons, and roads quiet enough to log a real ride or run.

The jewel within is the **El Dorado Nature Center** — 2-plus miles of trails winding around lakes, streams, grasslands, and forested pockets, including a quarter-mile paved loop and two dirt trails of about a mile each. The trails are open Tuesday through Sunday, 8 a.m. to 5 p.m. (no entry after 4:30), and the visitor center runs 8:30 a.m. to 4 p.m. Walking or biking in is free; only vehicles pay an entrance fee ($6 weekdays up to $9 on holidays), and it's now reachable by Long Beach Transit route 104. It's the best place in the city to bank a slow, restorative walk under tree cover — the kind of low-intensity movement that's easy to sustain and genuinely good for stress.

Directly to the west, **El Dorado Park West** is the active-sports counterpart across 275 acres: a 15-court tennis center, a skate park, a disc golf course, soccer and baseball fields, basketball courts, and a duck pond ringed by picnic lawns. It also hosts the Friday-night Municipal Band concerts all July.

## Recreation Park & Heartwell Park: Everyday Workout Grounds

Closer to the center of town near the Traffic Circle, **Recreation Park** is the everyday-athlete's park. It packs golf, two outdoor pools, a running track, a disc golf course, basketball and volleyball courts, and wide picnic lawns into a compact footprint, plus the band shell that hosts the Tuesday-evening Live at the Shell concert series starting July 14. The running track alone makes it worth the trip — a flat, measured surface for intervals is hard to find in a beach city.

Further east, **Heartwell Park** stretches 122 acres along Carson Street with a fishing pond, a dedicated bike/skate path, and — the standout feature — a **fitness course** with stations set along the walking paths. That combination of a long, safe path and built-in bodyweight stations turns an ordinary walk into a full circuit. On the Westside, **Wardlow Park** offers a community rec center, lighted basketball and roller-hockey courts, softball and soccer fields, and two playgrounds — a solid neighborhood option for families who want everyone moving in one place.

## Bluff Park & Bixby Park: Walk With an Ocean View

For the classic Long Beach walk, head to the shoreline. **Bluff Park**, running along Ocean Boulevard above Alamitos Beach, is where locals go at sunset. The loop is a little over a mile — flat along the bluff top, then down the stairways to the beach path and back up — with unbroken Pacific views and a steady sea breeze the whole way. It's one of the most beautiful and most repeatable walks in the city.

Just east, **Bixby Park** anchors the Alamitos Beach neighborhood with bluff-top seating, a new beach stairway, a walking path, and a twice-weekly farmers market. It's also home base for the free daily **Yoga on the Bluff** session at the Bixby Park Annex — 11 a.m. every day, with a second weekday session at 6 p.m. through October. Stacking a bluff walk with an open-air yoga class makes for one of the best no-cost mornings in Long Beach.

## Hilltop Park in Signal Hill: The Only Real Climb

Long Beach is famously flat, which is great for easy walks but short on the kind of incline that spikes your heart rate. The exception sits in the middle of it all: **Hilltop Park** in **Signal Hill**, the small city ringed by Long Beach. The short, steep climb up Skyline Drive — or the park's paved walking paths — earns you 360-degree views of the harbor, the downtown skyline, and, on a clear day, Catalina Island. Picnic tables and restrooms at the top make it a genuine destination rather than just a viewpoint. For anyone chasing a little cardio with a payoff at the summit, it's the best hill in the area.

## How to Build a Healthy Week Around Long Beach Parks

The trick isn't picking one park — it's rotating them. Use the El Dorado Nature Center for a calm midweek reset walk, Heartwell's fitness course or Recreation Park's track for a harder day, Bluff Park for the daily sunset loop, and Hilltop Park when you want a hill. Layer in the free summer programming — Municipal Band concerts four nights a week through July, Moonlight Movies on the sand on Tuesdays — and the parks quietly become your gym, your therapy walk, and your social calendar at once.
