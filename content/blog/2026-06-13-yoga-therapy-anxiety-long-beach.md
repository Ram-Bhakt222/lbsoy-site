## Anxiety Is a Long Beach Workforce Problem

Healthcare is the single largest job sector in Long Beach — MemorialCare Health System alone supports more than 8,600 local jobs — and hospitality and tourism is second, supporting roughly 18,650 jobs across the city's hotels, restaurants, and the convention district. Those two facts tell you something about the texture of stress here. A nurse working back-to-back shifts, a hotel front-desk lead during a sold-out convention week, a restaurant manager covering Belmont Shore's 2nd Street on a Friday night — these are not relaxed jobs. They are high-vigilance, irregular-hours, people-facing jobs, and the nervous system pays the bill.

With International Day of Yoga landing on June 21 — and 2026's official theme set as "Yoga for Healthy Ageing," fitting for a city with a large and growing older population — it's a good moment to look honestly at one question Long Beach residents ask us constantly: does yoga therapy actually help anxiety, or is that just wellness marketing?

Here's the careful answer.

## What the Clinical Evidence Shows About Yoga and Anxiety

The strongest data point is a 2020 randomized trial published in *JAMA Psychiatry* (Simon and colleagues), which enrolled 226 adults diagnosed with generalized anxiety disorder. Participants were assigned to twelve weeks of Kundalini yoga, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), or a stress-education control. Both yoga and CBT beat the control condition for reducing anxiety. CBT, however, outperformed yoga on the stricter statistical test, which is why the study's authors concluded that CBT should remain the first-line treatment — and that yoga is a reasonable, lower-barrier option that genuinely moves the needle.

A 2018 systematic review and meta-analysis in *Depression and Anxiety* (Cramer and colleagues) reached a compatible conclusion: there is evidence for small short-term reductions in anxiety, with the caveat that more high-quality trials are needed. That is an honest picture, and it is exactly the picture we want a Long Beach resident to have before they spend time or money on anything.

So the framing matters. Yoga therapy is not a cure for a clinical anxiety disorder, and it is not a replacement for therapy or medication when those are indicated. It is an evidence-supported adjunct — a structured, breath-led, body-based practice that helps regulate an over-activated stress response, and one that many people will stick with precisely because it doesn't feel like a clinical appointment.

## Why Yoga Therapy Is Different From a Drop-In Yoga Class

This distinction is the whole point, and it's worth being precise. A yoga class is a group movement session — accessible, social, and great for general wellbeing. Long Beach has a wonderful free one: Yogalution's Yoga on the Bluff runs every day at 11 a.m., year-round, at the Bixby Park Annex on Ocean Boulevard at Junipero Avenue, with a second 6 p.m. session added through spring and summer. Donations are welcome, never required. If you want a low-commitment on-ramp this week, that's it.

Yoga therapy is a different thing entirely. A yoga therapist is a clinically trained practitioner — at Long Beach School of Yoga, credentialed through the International Association of Yoga Therapists (IAYT) — who works one-on-one or in small condition-matched groups. The session is built around your nervous system and your history: a panic-symptom pattern, post-surgical recovery, hypertension, insomnia, caregiver burnout. The breathing protocols and movements are chosen for your physiology, not a class average, and they're adjusted over time. For someone managing real anxiety, that individualization is what separates a feel-good hour from a clinical adjunct.

## Anxiety Patterns We See Across Long Beach Neighborhoods

Three patterns come up again and again in our intake conversations.

The first is the **Downtown Long Beach desk-and-screen pattern** — office and finance workers whose anxiety lives in the breath. Shallow, fast, upper-chest breathing all day quietly keeps the sympathetic nervous system switched on. Yoga therapy here leans heavily on breath retraining and is one of the easiest things to fold into a workday.

The second is the **hospitality and healthcare shift pattern** — the irregular sleep and chronic vigilance of hotel, restaurant, and hospital staff across the city. The therapeutic priority is down-regulation and sleep, not exertion, and it's done in short, repeatable doses that survive a chaotic schedule.

The third is the **caregiver pattern**, often the adult children of older Long Beach residents — the 35-to-55-year-olds carrying their own jobs plus a parent's care. Their anxiety is anticipatory and rarely gets its own appointment. A standing one-on-one yoga therapy session is, for many of them, the only protected recovery hour in the week.

## A Realistic Way to Start This Summer

You don't need to choose between the free option and the clinical one — stack them. Use Yoga on the Bluff as the low-stakes habit that gets your body moving and your breath slower a few mornings a week. If your anxiety is persistent, sleep-disrupting, or interfering with work, pair that with a yoga therapy intake so a trained practitioner can build something specific to you. And if a clinician has recommended CBT or medication, keep that as the foundation — the evidence is clearest when yoga sits alongside first-line care, not in place of it.
