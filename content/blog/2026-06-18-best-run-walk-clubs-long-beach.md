Summer in Long Beach means one thing for active locals: the running season is in full swing. Whether you're a five-minute-mile competitor, a weekend jogger, or someone who prefers a social walk with friends, Long Beach has a run club, walk group, or group fitness community waiting for you. The beauty of group running is accountability — the group shows up, so you show up. And in a city like Long Beach, with miles of flat coastline and dozens of established training routes, the energy is genuinely electric once you find your people.

## Long Beach Run Club: The Main Event

The most established running community in Long Beach is **Long Beach Run Club**, which operates primarily through Meetup and maintains a steady Saturday crew. The club typically meets at the corner of Coronado Avenue and Ocean Boulevard (3300 East Ocean Boulevard), near the beach, and runs routes that average around 5 miles — adaptable for speed work or easy miles depending on the week. Saturday long runs are the flagship event, and the vibe is inclusive: runners of all paces are welcome, though the group typically runs together as a unit for the first mile, then breaks into pace groups.

What makes Long Beach Run Club stand out is consistency. The same faces show up week after week, which builds real camaraderie. There are no membership fees, no app requirements, and no pressure. Show up 10 minutes early, introduce yourself, and you're in.

## Run Club Girls: The Community Approach

If you're new to running or prefer a lower-pressure entry point, **Run Club Girls** operates in a slightly different vein. They offer two pace options at their 9 a.m. weekly runs: a "Sexy Pace" walk (2 miles, 18–20+ minutes per mile) and a "Sazón Pace" light jog (5K, 12–14 minutes per mile). The group emphasizes community over speed, and the demographic skews younger and more diverse than some traditional running clubs. There's a real sense of "we're all here to move and feel good," which resonates with people who are returning to fitness after a break or discovering running for the first time.

Run Club Girls also hosts special events throughout the year, including sunrise runs, trail running days, and post-run social hangouts. The barrier to entry is intentionally low.

## Lululemon Run Clubs: Free, Neighborhood-Based

Lululemon operates free run clubs at multiple Long Beach locations, particularly strong in **Belmont Shore** (near the 2nd & PCH store) and the broader East Long Beach corridor. These are more loosely structured than Long Beach Run Club but offer the advantage of proximity — if you live or work near Belmont Shore, there's likely a Lululemon run club within walking distance. The studios host weekly runs at varying paces and distances, and since they're free and open to the public, they're an easy first experience if you're testing the waters.

The Lululemon community skews toward younger, trendier demographics, but the welcoming attitude is consistent across their programs.

## Walking Groups: The Bluff Park & Naples Island Routes

For walkers, Long Beach has two standout routes that are perfect for group walking: the **Shoreline Pedestrian Bikepath** (4.5 miles, flat, car-free, paved) and the **Naples Island Canals Loop** (2.5 miles, gentle pace, residential waterfront scenery).

The Shoreline Pedestrian Bikepath runs from Shoreline Village all the way to Belmont Shore and is one of the most beautiful walking routes in Southern California. The separation of bikes and pedestrians means walkers don't feel squeezed, and the continuous ocean views make the miles pass quickly. Many Long Beach residents organize informal weekly walk groups on this path — often Tuesday or Thursday mornings — and you can easily start your own by posting on a local Nextdoor group or neighborhood Facebook page.

Naples Island's canals loop is shorter and more intimate. It's a favorite for couples and slower-paced groups, with plenty of photo-worthy moments (gondolas, Venice-like architecture, water views) that make the walk feel less like exercise and more like a neighborhood exploration.

## Gym-Based Group Fitness & Classes

For those who prefer structured group fitness, Long Beach has no shortage of options. **The Camp Transformation Center** (Downtown Long Beach, 245 The Promenade N) runs bootcamp-style group training starting at 5 a.m. weekdays — high-energy, community-driven, and designed for people who thrive on collective intensity. **Crunch Fitness** (100 Oceangate, Downtown) offers group cycling, yoga, Zumba, and HIIT classes throughout the day, with a vibrant community of regular participants. **Purple Yoga** (6535 E Pacific Coast Hwy) deserves mention here because while it's primarily a yoga studio, their hot yoga classes draw a deeply committed group fitness crew — over 125 classes per week, which means any time slot you choose, you'll find your people.

The advantage of gym-based group fitness is consistency and structure. Class times are set, instructors are certified, and there's a sense of ritual that works for many people. Many Long Beach residents who tried running clubs found that gym-based group fitness — particularly hot yoga or cycling — was their real calling.

## Larger Running Events: Spring the Conversation

If you want to test your fitness or join a larger community event, Long Beach hosts several running races throughout the year. **The Aloha Run** (September 20, Shoreline Aquatic Park) offers both 5K and 10K options plus a family-friendly walk option, and it's specifically designed to be accessible (no chip timing, no time cutoffs). **2XU Long Beach Marathon** (October 10–11) welcomes serious marathoners, casual runners, relay teams, and run club groups — many Long Beach clubs organize team entries, which is a fun way to commit with friends.

## How to Get Started

**Step 1: Pick your vibe.** Are you competitive or social? Fast or leisurely? Urban pavement or scenic trails? Your answer determines which group suits you.

**Step 2: Show up.** Most Long Beach run clubs have zero barriers to entry. Come 10 minutes early, make eye contact, and say hello. The regulars are always friendly to newcomers.

**Step 3: Commit to one group for four weeks.** This is key. The first run always feels awkward; by week four, you'll know the faces and the routes, and you'll actually be excited to show up.

**Step 4: Expand.** Once you've found your crew, you might cross-train with another group (maybe a walk club for recovery weeks) or explore a neighboring neighborhood's run club.

Long Beach's running and fitness community is genuinely welcoming. The infrastructure exists. You just have to show up.
